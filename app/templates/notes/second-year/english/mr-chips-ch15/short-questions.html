1. Why did Chips continue to teach his student during the air raid?

When the air raid started, Chips was taking his lower fourth in Latin on the ground floor of Brookfield. He continued to teach his student during the air raid because he thought that they would be safe there. The ground floor was pretty solidly built and was the best shelter for the students at that time.

2. How did boys feel, and how did Chips cheer up boys when the air raid began?

Some of the boys were nervous. Only a few able to be attentive as the bombs exploding quite near Brookfield. However, Mr. Chips cheered the boys, getting them to focus on their work. First the phrase 'stink merchant' and then, coincidentally, the reading of Latin history about some other German war cheered the whole class.

3. How did it look like as the explosions continued quite near Brookfield, and how much destruction did the shelling cause?

As the explosions continued quite near Brookfield, the whole building shook as if it were being lifted off its foundation. Afterwards, it was found that five bombs had fallen in and around Brookfield and nine persons had been killed.

4. Whom did Chips call 'Stink merchant'?

By 'Stink Merchant', Chips meant the scientists who had invented the lethal bombs in their laboratories. He used the phrase quite disgustingly. However, this broke titters of nervous laughter in the class, because it was Burrow, the pale, lean and medically unfit scientist who was nicknamed the 'Stink Merchant'.

5. When did Mr. Chips resign from his job for the second time?

On November 11th 1918, Chips sent his resignation to the Board of Governors for the second time. Chips resigned for the second time probably because he thought it was over for as the war had also ended. The next day he was in bed with Bronchitis and stayed there till after Christmas.

6. Why was there no farewell or presentation when he left the school for the second time?

There was no farewell or presentations, as Chips himself requested not to conduct any such event. He only shook hands with his successor and the word 'acting' was crossed out on official stationery.

7. Which class was Chips taking when bombs began falling around?

Chips was taking his lower fourth in Latin on the ground floor of School House, when the guns began instantly.

8. Why did Chips not run for the dug-out while there was a plenty of shelling?

As there was plenty of shrapnel falling outside, it seemed to Chips that they might as well stay where they were, on the ground floor of School House. It was pretty solidly built and made as good as Brookfield could offer.

9. Which lesson of Latin was Chips teaching in the loud roar of bombing?

Chips was teaching the affairs of Caesar in Gaul and irregualr conjugation of verb 'tollo'. He said to one of his pupil, Robertson, "It may possibly seem to you ‐ at this particular moment in the world's history ‐ that affairs of Caesar in Gaul, some two thousand years ago, are of somewhat secondary importance."

10. Were the students paying any heed to what Chips was teaching in the rain of shells?

Some of the boys were nervous; only few were able to be attentive. Speaking a little louder amid the echoing thunders of the guns and the shrill noise of anti-aircraft shells, Chips continued teaching the lower fourth.

11. Who was Buffles?

Buffles was a pale, lean, and medically unfit science master who was nicknamed 'Stink Master'.

12. What did Chips say to the students when a bomb fell quite near them?

Chips said, "Let us resume our work. If it is fate that we are soon to be interrupted, let us be found employing ourselves in some thing really appropriate."

13. Who got ready to construe when it was shelling all around? OR What sort of boy was Maynard?

Marynard was a chubby, dauntless, clever, and impudent boy. When it was shelling all around, he got ready to construe.

14. What was the reaction of Chips when the story of his bravery during the shelling was told again and again? OR What was the story told about Chips after the event of shelling about Brookfield?

The story was told, retold, embellished. The dear old boy did not turn a hair. Even, he found some old tag to illustrate how the Germans were fighting.

15. What happened on 11th November, 1918?

News came in the morning about the end of the war. A whole holiday was decreed for the school, and the kitchen staff were implored to provide as cheerful a spread as wartime rationing permitted. There was much cheering and singing and bread fight across the Dining Hall.

16. What happened when Chips came in the hall during the uproar of the celebrations at the end of World War I?

When Chips entered in the midst of uproar, there was an instant hush. Everyone gazed on him with eager, as on a symbol of victory.
