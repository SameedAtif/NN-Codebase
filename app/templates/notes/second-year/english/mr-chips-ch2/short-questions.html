1. Describe the outlook of Brookfield.

Brookfield lay across the road behind a defensive wall of ancient elms. The building of the school looked reddish brown under its autumn covering of creeper. The building was centered upon a quadrangle. There were acres of playing-fields beyond.

2. When was Brookfield established?

Brookfield was an old foundation. It was established in the reign of Elizabeth, as a grammar school.

3. What was the status of Brookfield? Why couldn't it become as famous as Harrow?

Brookfield was a good school of second rank. It might have become as famous as Harrow, but its luck had not been so good. At times like the Napoleonic wars, it was almost non-existent and at other times, very famous.

Wetherby restored its fortunes somewhat, but its subsequent history never raised it to the front-rank status.

4. When was Brookfield rebuilt and extended?

The main structure of Brookfield was rebuilt and large additions were made during the reign of the first George.

5. What kind of people did Brookfield produce?

Brookfield produced a fair number of important men of the age ‐ judges, members of Parliament, colonial administrators, a few peers and bishops. Mostly it turned out merchants, manufacturers, and professional men.

6. How were Chips and Brookfield similar?

Brookfield and Chips were similar in many ways. Mr. Chips, in any social or academic sense, was just no more brilliant than Brookfield itself. Both of them were average in status.

7. What was the ambition of Mr. Chips?

Chips was a very ambitious teacher in his twenties . He was as ambitious as any other young man. His dream had been to get a headship, or a senior mastership in a first class school.

8. How did Chips come to know his limitations? What were his limitations?

Chips was very ambitious in his twenties. He was so ambitious that it took him a long time to realize his limitations. It was only after repeated trials and failures that he realized his limitations gradually.

His qualifications were inadequate. His limitations were his mediocre degree, his unreliable discipline, lack of private means and family connections of any importance.

9. Describe Mr. Chips as an institution at Brookfield?

We can look upon Mr. Chips as an institution at Brookfield. Serving Brookfield for decades, he achieved almost every glory he wanted to. He was the guest of honour at Brookfieldian dinners and court of appeal in all matters that could affect the history and tradition of Brookfield.

10. Give the highlights of Mr. Chips' career.

At forty, he was rooted, settled, and quite happy. At fifty he was the doyen of the staff. At sixty, the guest of honour at old Brookfieldian dinners, the court of appeal in all matters.

11. When did Mr. Chips retire and what was he presented with?

Mr. Chips retired in 1913, when he turned sixty-five. He was presented with a cheque, a writing desk and a clock.
