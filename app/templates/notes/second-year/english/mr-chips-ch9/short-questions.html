1. Where did Mr. Chips start living after the death of Katherine?

After the death of Katherine, Mr. Chips started living in his old bachelor quarter. He shifted there for his commodious apartment in School House.

2. Chips thought at first he would give up his house mastership. Why didn't he do so and how did he feel afterwards?

Chips thought at first to give up his house mastership, but the head persuaded him otherwise. Later, he was glad as the work gave him something to do. It filled up an emptiness in his mind and heart.

3. What was the effect of Katherine's death on Chips?

The death of Katherine made Mr. Chips a different man. Just as marriage had added something, so did the bereavement. After the great shock, he became the kind of man whom boys classed as 'old'. On the other hand, he became more mature, more confident, more gentle and inimitable.

4. How did the death of Katherine affect Mr. Chips physically? OR How fit was Mr. Chips physically in his fifties?

Mr. Chips was quite fit in his fifties. The death of Katherine did have a considerable physical impact on him. Although he was still active, keen and energetic, yet the boys now classed him as 'old'. He could still knock up a half century on the cricket field and play as well as many a fellow half of his age.

5. What did Naylor say about Chips, and how did Chips, when he was over eighty felt about Naylor?

Naylor had called Chips 'old' when the latter was fifty. When Chips was eighty, he used to tell that incident with many chuckles. He used to wonder if Naylor, who himself must have been fifty now, still thought that fifty was an age to be called 'old'.

6. What changes appeared in Mr. Chips with the new century?

With the new century, as Chips turned fifty, a certain maturity settled upon him that gathered all his developing mannerisms. No longer did he have disciplinary troubles or feel shy about his own work and worth.

7. What did long years of service at Brookfield give Mr. Chips in return?

When Chips was over fifty, he found that his pride in Brookfield reflected back. Now, he started feeling pride in himself and his position. The freedom of his work had acquired him a unique status.

8. What eccentricities had Mr. Chips acquired in his fifties?

One of his gentle eccentricities was the wearing of a tattered gown and the other was specific way of looking while taking call-over. Mr. Chips used to wear his gown till it was too tattered to hold together and his look was a favourite subject to mimicry among the boys. Mr. Chips used to wear steel rimmed spectacles which were slipping down his nose.

9. How did Mr. Chips take the call-over?

Mr. Chips used to take the call-over with an air of mystic abandonment to a ritual. He used to stand on the wooden bench by big hall steps. He held the long school-list curling over a board. Each boy used to speak his own name as he passed. Mr. Chips would then verify and tick his name on the list.

10. How far had Katherine's intellect and the brilliance of her mind influence Mr. Chips?

Katherine had not been able, in so brief a time, to impart all her intellect and brilliance of her mind to Mr. Chips. Nevertheless, she had left him with a calmness and poise that harmonized with his own inward emotions.

11. Was Mr. Chips a pro-Boer, and what did he think of those people who were pro-Boers?

Mr. Chips was not a pro-Boer ‐ he was too traditional for that, but he did not share the general fanatic bitterness against the Boers. He disliked the kind of people who were pro-Boers.

12. Why did Mr. Chips not share the general jingo bitterness against Boers?

Mr. Chips did not shared the general jingo bitterness against the Boers because sometimes he felt a curious similarity between English and the Boers. He would feel that Boers were engaged in a struggle that had a peculiar resemblence with those of English.

13. What did Mr. Chips say to Lloyd George when the latter came as the guest of honour to a Brookfield Speech Day?

Lloyd George was a Prime Minister of Britain. When he came as the guest of honour to a Brookfield Speech Day. Chips said L.G. seemed to have improved a great deal. L.G. laughed heartily at this and talked to Chips more than to anyone else.

14. What did Chips remember about Halsbury?

About Halsbury, Chips remembered that he was a lawyer, and lawyers, he thought, lived longer. Chips recalled that Halsbury was made Chancellor at eighty-two and he died at the age of ninety.
