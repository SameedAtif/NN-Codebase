1. Who was Mrs. Wickett and what did she do for Mr. Chips?

Mrs. Wickett had been in charge of the linen-room at Brookfield. She was also the landlady of Mr. Chips. She let a room to Mr.Chips after his retirement from the school, and looked after him as a kind-hearted landlady.

2. What kind of life did Mr. Chips live at Mrs. Wickett's?

Mr. Chips lived a quite and peaceful life at Mrs. Wickett's. He had his own quite enjoyments and his past. He had no worries.

3. Write a note on Mr. Chips' financial position.

Mr. Chips had a sound financial position. His pension was adequate, and there was a little money saved up besides. He had invested his money in gilt-edged stocks. He could afford everything and anything he wanted. He had no financial worries.

4. How was Mr. Chips' room furnished?

Mr. Chips' room was furnished simply and with school-masterly taste. There were a few bookshelves and sporting trophies, a mantelpiece full of fixture cards and pictures of Arcopolis and Forum on the wall, a worn Turkey carpet and big easy-chairs.

5. What kind of books were read by Mr. Chips?

The books Mr. Chips read were cheifly classical. There were, however, a few books on history and some famous literary works. There was also a bottom shelf piled with a lot of detective novels.

Mr. Chips enjoyed reading almost all kinds of books he had in his room. Sometimes he took down Virgil or Xenophon and read for a few moments, but he was soon back again with detective novels.

6. What did Mr. Chips think of Latin and Greek languages?

Mr. Chips thought of Latin and Greek as dead languages. According to him, English gentlemen ought to know only a few quotations from these languages.

7. What kind of classical scholar was Mr. Chips?

Mr. Chips was not a very profound classical scholar. Although he had been teaching classical language assiduously for many years, he couldn't become a great scholar.

8. What did Mr. Chips like in 'The Times'?

Mr. Chips liked those short leading articles in 'The Times' that introduced a few tagged classical languages. These tags could be understood and recognized only by him and few other fellows of his age.

9. What were the routine activites of Mr. Chips at Mrs. Wickett's?

Mr. Chips lived a peaceful life at Mrs. Wickett's. He lived a fairly active life. He drank tea, received callers, and wrote his occasional letters. He invited new masters to tea, as well as new boys.

10. Why was it oddly incorrect to say that Mr. Chips was a typical bachelor?

It was oddly incorrect to say Mr. Chips a bachelor because he was not a bachelor at all. He had married, though it was so long ago that none of the staff at Brookfield could remember his wife.

11. How was the room given to Chips by Mrs. Wickett?

The room which Mrs. Wickett gave to Chips was small but very comfortable and sunny. Chips felt much convenient because his room was near Brookfield.

12. What would Mr. Chips do in the afternoon in mild weather when he lived at Mrs. Wickett's house?

Mr. Chips would love to stroll across the playing fields in an afternoon and watch the games only in mild weather because he was suffering from asthma. He liked to smile and exchange a few words with the boys when they touched their caps to him.

13. How did the guests feel while watching Chips make tea?

Chips always entertained his guests with tea and walnut cake with pink icing from Reddaway's. He made tea by mixing careful spoonfuls from different caddies. The guests found it fun to watch him make tea.

14. What would Chips ask the new boys who came to see him?

Chips would ask the boys who came to see him, where they lived, and if they had any family connections to Brookfield. He would love to guide them and make them familiar with the environment of Brookfield.

15. What would Chips say to Mrs. Wickett when she came to clear the remains of the tea party?

When the boys after staying with Chips for almost one hour went away to school and Mrs. Wickett came to clear the remains of the tea party, Chips told her about his guests. He shared with her the discussion which he had with the students. He informed her that he had a very interesting time with the students.

16. What did Mr. Chips tell Mrs. Wickett about Major Collingwood?

Mr. Chips told Mrs. Wickett, when she came to clean up the remains of the tea party in his room, that one of the boys named Branksome was the nephew of Major Collingwood. Collingwood was the boy who was punished by Chips for climbing on to the gymnasium roof to get a ball out of a gutter. He was killed in Egypt during the war.

17. What did Mrs. Wickett tell about Collingwood?

Mrs. Wickett told Mr. Chips that she knew Collingwood who was cheeky to her generally. But they uttered never bad words to each other. He was just a cheeky sort of boy and meant no harm. She asked Chips if Collingwood was the boy who got the medal.

18. With what the mantelpiece was crowded in Mr. Chips' room after retirement?

The mantelpiece in Mr. Chips' room after retirement was crowded with fixture-cars and signed photographs of boys and men. The internal setting of his room represented school-masterly taste.

19. What subjects did Chips teach and how did he teach them?

Latin, Greek and Roman history were the subjects which were taught by Mr. Chips. He considered Latin and Greek more than dead languages from which the English men ought to learn a few quotations. He loved to teach classical education. Being a conservative person, Chips liked to teach in his old method and pronounciation.
